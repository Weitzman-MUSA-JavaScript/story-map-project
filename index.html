What is an Atoll?

The story of an Atoll starts with a volcanic island that pops up from the ocean, formed by magma from deep inside the Earth. As this island settles, it becomes a home for life, with tiny coral creatures attaching to its edges and starting to build a reef. Over thousands of years, while the island itself wears down and starts to sink back into the sea, the coral keeps growing upwards, reaching for the sunlight it needs to survive. Eventually, the island disappears under the water, leaving behind a ring-shaped barrier of coral that surrounds a calm lagoon. This lagoon, shallow and full of marine life, is bordered by the reef and sometimes has little islands made of coral bits and sand. This completes the transformation into the beautiful atoll we see today, a perfect circle of life and nature’s craft.

Image by: USFWS

Approximately 90% of all atolls are Oceanic, which means they developed around submerged volcanoes in deep waters, as opposed to continental shelves. The Pacific Ocean hosts the majority of atolls as well as the largest combined area of atolls.

The Maldives, a tropical paradise in the Indian Ocean, is the nation with the Largest Total Atoll Area in the world. It consists of 22 natural oceanic atolls, which are coral reef formations that have developed around submerged volcanoes over millions of years. These atolls, characterized by their shallow central lagoons and vibrant coral ecosystems, are home to over 1,000 islands. The Maldives' unique geography supports an extraordinary diversity of marine life, making it a global hotspot for coral reefs, snorkeling, and scuba diving. As the world's lowest-lying country, the Maldives is highly vulnerable to sea-level rise, highlighting the importance of preserving its delicate atoll ecosystems.

The Tuamotu Archipelago, located in French Polynesia, is the region with the Highest Number of Atolls in the world, comprising nearly 60 distinct atolls spread across the Pacific Ocean. These atolls, like other oceanic atolls, formed around submerged volcanic islands over millions of years and are characterized by their coral reef structures that enclose shallow, turquoise lagoons. The largest atoll, Rangiroa, is one of the world’s largest, providing a hub for marine life and tourism. The isolation and vast area of the Tuamotu atolls contribute to their unique ecosystems and rich biodiversity.
Entrance to the lagoon of Rangiroa Atoll. Photo Credit: Wikimedia

The Great Chagos Bank, located in the Indian Ocean within the British Indian Ocean Territory, is the Largest Atoll in the World. Covering an area of around 12,000 square kilometers, it comprises a ring of coral reefs enclosing a vast central lagoon, though much of the reef is submerged. Unlike typical atolls with numerous islands, the Great Chagos Bank features fewer land areas but supports significant biodiversity, with coral reefs that are home to a wide variety of marine species. Three small islands on the Three Brothers group on the western Great Chagos Bank. Photo Credit: Brian W Bowen

Thiladhunmathi Atoll, located in the northern part of the Maldives, holds the distinction of having the Largest Lagoon Area among all atolls. Spanning both Haa Alif and Haa Dhaalu administrative regions, this atoll is characterized by its expansive, shallow lagoon, which supports a wide range of marine biodiversity. The vast lagoon area, dotted with coral reefs and small islands, offers a rich environment for fishing, marine tourism, and traditional livelihoods.
Resorts on Thiladhunmathi Atoll's islands. Photo Credit: Travel Trade Maldives

Kiritimati, also known as Christmas Island, is located in the central Pacific Ocean and is the atoll with the Largest Land Area in the world. Unlike most atolls, which have minimal land and are primarily composed of coral reefs surrounding a lagoon, Kiritimati possesses about 400 square kilometers of land, making it exceptionally large for an atoll. As part of the Republic of Kiribati, this atoll supports unique ecosystems, including coastal lagoons, wetlands, and significant bird habitats.

Atolls are Like Hidden Gems of the Ocean,

Not just because they're beautiful, but because they are crucial for marine life and the communities that depend on them. These rings of coral create ideal environments for marine species, like fish and sea turtles, by providing shelter, nutrition and the perfect temperature for life to thrive. Additionally, atolls serve as natural shields, protecting nearby islands from storms.
However, they are fragile ecosystems, highly vulnerable to climate change and rising sea levels, which threaten their stability. Poor soil and constant erosion make farming nearly impossible on these islands, forcing communities to rely on imported resources. As a result, industries like fisheries and satellite launch sites have become vital for their economies. Despite their beauty and ecological importance, many remote atolls were used as nuclear testing grounds in the 20th century, highlighting the risk their isolated nature presents. Today, efforts are being made to restore these atolls and promote sustainable practices to safeguard their fragile ecosystems. ​
